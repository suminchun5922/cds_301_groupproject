üìù Abstract

This project investigates the ongoing shift in fashion retail by analyzing both macro-level market trends and micro-level consumer behaviors from two datasets. U.S. Census data shows that while traditional clothing retail grew moderately from 1999 to 2023, the e-commerce sector expanded exponentially, recording over 1,200% growth.

A consumer-level analysis of 3,900 online purchase records reveals that this surge is not limited to the younger generation. The data identifies a broad engagement across all age groups (20s‚Äì60s), challenging the stereotype that online shopping is solely for the youth. 'Clothing' remains the dominant category. Furthermore, spending was found to be age-independent, while December consistently showed seasonal spikes, highlighting the importance of timing in online fashion sales.

üéØ Conclusion

This project provides a data-driven confirmation of the structural shift in U.S. retail. The transition from physical to digital is quantified by the massive growth in e-commerce sales (Fig 1 & 6), establishing it as a permanent restructuring of the market rather than a temporary trend.

Our findings highlight three strategic pillars for success. First, Clothing accounts for nearly half of all online transactions, validating its role as the primary market driver. Second, contrary to the 'youth-centric' stereotype, purchasing power is evenly distributed across all age demographics, indicating a universal adoption of e-commerce. Finally, the recurring December sales spikes prove that seasonality is the critical determinant of annual revenue performance.

For retailers and marketers, the data suggests a clear path forward: Prioritize fashion categories, adopt an age-inclusive marketing strategy, and optimize operations for the year-end holiday peak to align with proven consumer behaviors.
